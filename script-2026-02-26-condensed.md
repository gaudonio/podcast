**A:** Moving to something practical. Greg Isenberg shared a framework for using Obsidian plus Claude Code as a 24/7 personal operating system.

**B:** Ten-step process: Write everything in markdown. Daily notes, projects, beliefs, meetings. Link notes so they mirror how your brain thinks. Install Obsidian CLI so Claude Code can read your entire vault.

**A:** The custom slash commands: `/context` loads your full state. `/trace` shows how ideas evolved. `/connect` bridges domains. `/ideas` generates startup concepts from your vault. `/graduate` promotes thoughts to assets.

**B:** Golden rule: Human writes the vault, agents read and execute. Don't re-explain projects — use reference files.

**A:** He says markdown files are the oxygen of LLMs. The more you write, the more context your agents have. Once the vault exists, the agent stops being generic. It thinks in your voice.

**B:** 99.99% won't do this because it requires reflection. That's why it becomes a moat.

**A:** Speaking of moats, Johann Sath compared default OpenClaw to his setup after three weeks.

**B:** Default: Basic workspace, skills folder. His version: Custom identity files, live working memory, long-term memory, autonomous thinking loop, client profiles, decision frameworks, writing voice guide, startup rules, daily logs, custom skills like tweet-writer and security-auditor, plus content, consulting, drafts, CRM folders.

**A:** His line: "This is the difference between a chatbot and an AI employee. None of this is built in. Your setup is your moat."

**B:** Which ties to Dan Shipper's thread on what personal software actually is.

**A:** Shipper says it's not vibe-coded SaaS — that's still a skill most don't have. Instead, personal software is an agent you have a relationship with. You teach it, shape it. It grows personality in response to you.

**B:** At his company, almost everyone has a claw. Your claw becomes a mirror — if you're great at growth, your claw is too. If you're a writer, your agent cares about sentences.

**A:** Trust builds through daily use. Because you depend on it, it writes trustable code. And trust transfers — if I trust your claw, others will too.

**B:** He also talks specialization. Teams of agents are the future — not one agent to rule them all. Agents mirror org charts. Everyone gets a deputy that promotes them out of their current job.

**A:** When a few people have agents, you need a Sheriff — an agent with more permissions that manages access, sets rules, orients new agents.

**B:** His summary: Personal software is code that writes itself through your relationship with an agent, conforming to your personality and taste.

**A:** Pulling this together: Shift from typing syntax to describing intent. From generic assistants to personalized agents. From tools to systems that mirror how we think.

**B:** The throughline is relationship. Best setups aren't about features — they're where human and agent built trust. The agent becomes an extension of you.
